# Writing Guidelines: Style and Format

Guidelines for the text quality and style of product design case studies — focused on how content reads, not on narrative structure or section order.

---

## Agent behaviour when writing content

- Ask before guessing. If context is ambiguous, a decision has more than one valid path, or key details (metrics, team size, timeline, outcomes) are missing — ask rather than invent.
- Do not fabricate: no invented metrics, user counts, team names, or outcomes. If information is not provided, ask for it or write around the gap explicitly.
- If a structural or editorial decision is genuinely hard to make without more context, name the options and ask which the author prefers.

---

## How case studies are actually read

- Readers scan first: they read headings, subheadings, the first sentence of each paragraph, and any highlighted content. Full reading only happens if scanning caught their interest.
- The decision to keep reading is made in the first 30–60 seconds — based on whether the product, problem, and outcome are immediately clear.
- Hiring managers and design managers review many case studies in parallel. They will not work to extract meaning from dense or ambiguous writing.

---

## Text length and paragraph structure

- One paragraph, one idea. If a paragraph can be split without losing meaning — split it.
- Maximum 3–4 sentences per paragraph. Long paragraphs get skipped.
- Short, dense paragraphs signal substance. Long paragraphs signal filler.
- Use bullet lists for genuinely enumerable items, but do not overuse them. If points are logically connected, one tight sentence beats three weak bullets.
- Keep any single bullet list to a maximum of 5–6 items.

---

## Headings and subheadings

- A heading should communicate something about the content, not just label a stage.
  - Stronger: *Why the onboarding flow was losing users at step 3*
  - Weaker: *Research*
- Headings should work as a standalone sequence: when scanned top to bottom, they should tell a coherent story.
- Do not use question-form headings as a stylistic device — they create a long-read feel and add no information.

---

## Voice and person

- Write in first person, active voice: "I ran", "We decided" — not "analysis was conducted", "a decision was made."
- Do not hide behind passive or impersonal constructions when describing your own decisions. It makes your contribution invisible.
- Distinguish "I" from "we" consistently: if a decision was the team's — "we"; if it was yours — "I". Mixing them erodes credibility.

---

## Verbs and specificity of action

- Use verbs that describe a concrete action, not general participation.
  - Stronger: "ran 8 user interviews", "proposed an alternative flow to the PM", "negotiated scope reduction with engineering"
  - Weaker: "participated in research", "worked with the team", "was involved in the process"
- Every claim should be verifiable or illustratable.
  - Stronger: "6 out of 8 participants could not find the notification settings"
  - Weaker: "users were confused by the settings"
- Specific numbers — even small ones — are more convincing than vague quantities: "3 prototype iterations" beats "several iterations".
- Where metrics are not available, describe observable behaviour changes or qualitative shifts. Do not replace absent data with generalisations about "improved experience."

---

## Tone

- Confident but not self-congratulatory. State what happened and what changed — do not editorialize about how significant it was.
- Describe difficulties and trade-offs honestly. A case study with no tension is not credible.
- Do not write in a promotional tone. A case study is an account of thinking under real constraints, not a pitch for the product.
- Do not over-explain decisions defensively. State the context, state what you chose, state why — then move on.

---

## Human voice

The writing should sound like a product designer talking to a peer — direct, informed, occasionally dry. Not a press release, not a stakeholder update, not a product manager's roadmap.

- Vary sentence length deliberately. A short sentence hits harder after a longer one.
- Write as though the reader is smart and time-poor: give them what they need to understand, not what makes the work sound impressive.
- Avoid corporate register: "leveraged", "delivered value", "drove impact", "stakeholder-aligned" signal performance review language, not honest reflection.
- Use "I" and "we" naturally — not "the team" or "the project". Real people write about their work in first person.

---

## Jargon and buzzwords

- Prefer the simpler word when it carries the same meaning.
- Do not use trend vocabulary as a substitute for description. Words like "holistic", "user-centric", "data-driven", "impactful", or "seamless" mean nothing without the specific content they should be describing.
- If you find yourself writing a buzzword, ask: what exactly does this mean in this context? Write that instead.

---

## Abbreviations and domain terminology

Not all readers share the same domain background. A hiring manager at a fintech company may not know what LMS means; a recruiter at an edtech company may not know what MRR means.

- Spell out any abbreviation on first use: "Learning Management System (LMS)", "Net Promoter Score (NPS)".
- If a technical term is central to the case study, define it briefly in context — one clause is enough.
- Do not assume that because a term is common in your industry, it is common to the reader.

---

## Common text anti-patterns

- **Nominalisation instead of verbs:** "the conducting of an analysis" → "I analysed"
- **Passive voice to avoid authorship:** "the design was developed" → by whom?
- **Filler constructions that pad sentences:** "within the scope of this project", "based on the above", "which allowed us to"
- **Clichés that replace specific content:** "user at the centre", "iterative approach", "data-driven design" — only use these if you immediately follow with a concrete example
- **Monotonous rhythm:** starting every paragraph or bullet with the same word or structure

---

## Patterns that signal AI-generated text

These patterns make writing sound assembled rather than authored. Avoid them.

- **Em dash overuse:** use a comma, period, or parenthesis in most cases instead
- **Significance inflation:** "pivotal moment", "testament to", "marks a shift" — if it is not literally pivotal, do not call it that
- **Copula avoidance:** "serves as", "stands as", "functions as" → use "is" or "are"
- **Rule of three forced:** "clear, concise, and actionable" — if two elements make the point, stop at two
- **Filler transitions:** "Additionally,", "Furthermore,", "It is worth noting that" — cut or rephrase
- **Announcing instead of doing:** "Let's explore...", "Here's what you need to know" — just write the content
- **Generic conclusions:** "the future looks bright", "exciting times ahead" — end on a specific observation or fact
- **Consecutive bullets with the same opening word or structure**
- **Bolding phrases inside body text without reason**

---

## The specificity test

Before publishing, check every achievement or outcome statement against this test:

> Does it contain at least **two** of the following three elements?
> 1. A number or metric
> 2. A named tool, method, or framework
> 3. A named context — product, team, user segment, or constraint

- Fails: *Improved team efficiency*
- Passes: *Reduced component creation time from 3 days to 4 hours across 8 product teams by establishing a tokenised design system in Figma*

AI tools cannot supply these details. Only the author can.

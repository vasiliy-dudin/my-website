# Quality Criteria for CV Content, Case Studies, and Website Copy

## The core test for any piece of content

**The specificity test:** Every claim should have at least two of these three elements:
- A number or metric (e.g., "reduced onboarding time by 40%", "adopted by 8 product teams")
- A named tool, method, or framework (e.g., "using design tokens in Figma", "through moderated usability testing with 12 participants")
- A named context (e.g., "across a B2B platform with 200K+ business users", "during a zero-to-one redesign of the billing flow")

"Improved the user experience" fails the test. "Reduced task completion time from 7 minutes to 90 seconds for a complex B2B onboarding flow, measured through unmoderated usability testing" passes it.

---

## Criteria for strong CV bullet points

A strong bullet point:
- Opens with a specific, concrete action verb — not a generic one
- Describes scope and context, not just activity
- Reflects the designer's actual contribution, distinguishable from what the team did
- Includes outcome or impact (quantified where possible; qualitative where metrics don't exist)
- Uses vocabulary that matches how the role is described in target job descriptions
- Would make sense to a hiring manager who doesn't know the company or product

Weak action verbs to avoid: worked on, helped, supported, assisted, contributed to, involved in, responsible for
Strong action verbs by domain:
- Design systems: Architected, Systematised, Standardised, Governed, Scaled, Documented, Migrated
- Accessibility: Audited, Remediated, Certified, Established (standards/guidelines)
- Research and validation: Prototyped, Validated, Synthesised, Facilitated, Recruited
- Product design: Designed, Defined, Scoped, Mapped, Tested, Shipped, Iterated
- Collaboration/leadership: Led, Aligned, Presented, Mentored, Coordinated

## Criteria for a strong professional summary

A strong summary:
- Names the specific type of designer (not just "experienced product designer")
- Specifies the type of products and problems they work on (B2B SaaS, design systems, etc.)
- States the value they bring in concrete terms (cross-functional bridge, accessibility expertise, frontend fluency, etc.)
- Is 3 lines or fewer
- Would not fit any other designer without changing at least half the words

A weak summary is interchangeable across dozens of designers, uses clichés, and makes vague claims about impact.

## Criteria for strong portfolio case study content

A strong case study:
- Makes the problem clear before presenting any solution — specifically, why it mattered to users and to the business
- Shows the designer's own thinking and decisions, not just the team's output
- Explains what options were considered and why one was chosen over others
- Shows evidence of contact with users (research, testing, feedback) — even if lightweight
- Describes constraints honestly: time, technical, organisational, resource
- Shows real outcomes — not "the stakeholders were happy" but something that changed in user behaviour, product metrics, or business results
- Is scannable: a hiring manager should be able to get the story in under 2 minutes without reading every word

A weak case study shows: a brief → wireframes → final screens, with no explanation of decisions, no evidence of research, and no outcomes.

## Criteria for strong website copy (About page, hero, project cards)

**Hero/intro statement:**
- Says who you are and what problems you solve in one or two sentences
- Is specific to you — not a generic senior designer description
- Does not use clichés or buzzwords
- Works for a hiring manager who knows nothing about you

**About page:**
- Describes your actual design approach in specific terms (not "I love solving complex problems")
- Mentions the types of teams and products you work best in
- Is written in first person, sounds like a person talking
- Is under 200 words — detail lives in case studies

**Project card descriptions (for the portfolio grid):**
- Follows the formula: problem → role → outcome in 2–3 sentences
- Is specific enough that someone knows what the project was about without clicking
- Does not oversell or use marketing language

## Signals that distinguish senior from mid-level work

Hiring managers use portfolios and CVs to calibrate seniority. These signals correlate with senior-level positioning:

Senior signals:
- Evidence of ambiguity handled: unclear brief, no precedent, competing stakeholder needs
- Cross-functional influence: changed how a team worked, not just what it built
- Systems thinking: decisions that account for edge cases, future states, scale
- Mentoring or leading other designers (even informally)
- Working without constant direction, proposing solutions rather than executing requests
- Talking about trade-offs and what was left on the table, not just what was shipped
- Understanding of engineering and business constraints — not just user needs

Mid-level signals (avoid over-emphasising at senior level):
- Execution quality on defined tasks
- Following an established process
- Individual screen-level design decisions
- Tool proficiency

## Red flags in content quality

These patterns appear consistently in weak applications and should be corrected:
- All bullets describe activity with no outcome
- Summary describes personality traits instead of professional positioning
- Portfolio shows polished UI with no explanation of decisions or context
- Language that could describe any designer at any company
- Claiming "led" or "drove" work that was clearly a team effort with no specific contribution noted
- Using "passionate" or equivalent — signals generic application, not genuine fit
- Every case study has a happy ending with no mention of constraints, failures, or learnings
